Before the journey can start, one must determine where they're heading to, what dangers may lay along the way, and how they can get there.

Where?

First and foremost, where are the players heading? How far are they going?
This determines how much the party will encounter along the way, what hurdles they'll have to head through, and when they might be able to pit-stop at some civilization or other stronghold.
Inspired by Pointy Hat's Travelling Event System, this system counts distance in general distances, being close, far, and very far, which can be determined in terms of distance, danger and importance. Note, if the destination is close enough or peaceful enough, you may not need to run this system, such as walking across the city, or a ferry ride, just simply describe it away, and perhaps let the players roleplay what its like across the ride. Cycles, inspired by the Dungeon Master's Guide, determine the steps the party must go through to reach the steps, as will be covered later in this page.
Distance Example(s) Amount of Cycles
Close A supply run to a nearby town, trekking to a popular shrine up a mountain. 1
Far A supply run to a major city, a temple located high in the mountains. 2
Very Far Transporting a rare item to nobility, an ancient dungeon once inhabited by monks - said to hold great treasures. 3

How?

The second part of travelling is how the party is going to get where they need to go, will they need to procure extra matierials before they head to their destination, such as something to resist the heat, a person to navigate them, a ship, etc?
If the players get something to only help them on their way, perhaps that could reduce the time it takes, or the danger the route would normally impose, such as a trek across the dessert becoming a Close distance instead of a Very Far distance because they rented a sand buggy.
If the players do not bring the matierials they need, how does that affect their survival? Of course, let them know the difficulty before throwing them to the wolves, but if they go through with it, let them feel their choice, whether that be through difficulty or time. For example, if the players do not get a guide to cross through the dessert, perhaps they run across a sandworm den, when the guide would've known to avoid it. As well, lets say the players didn't buy a map to cross through the jungle, and ended up wasting a bunch of time getting lost, well, that gave the BBEG or the environment itself to progress in the party's absence, turning the town they were heading to into a zombie town.

As well, this doesn't have to be constant, such as travelling between towns, you could instead only need this during more intense travels, such as crossing a desert, consider:
"The Sands (the desert the players have to cross) is a challenge even to those used to the heat, if you're going to cross it, you're going to need the correct supplies to do so." This way, you can even make it a quest to gather the nessesary supplies to cross to where your BBEG may be hiding, raising the stakes, and giving the players a question of "if we get the supplies, will we have enough time before the BBEG (blows up that town?)"

Journey Cycles

A journey with this system takes place in cycles, where your players get to explore, problem-solve, potentially battle, and roleplay.

A typical journey cycle is resolved in this order:
  1. Environment
  2. Navigate
  3. Encounter
  4. Downtime
The first part to figure out a travel cycle is what the environment is like: what biome you're travelling through, the weather for the day, and any other hazards that could impede the journey, as well as give it a mood.

Hazards

Many hazards can impede the player's journey through an area, particularly weather. These hazards can be strewn through many miles or stay for only one cycle.
For example, a desert may stay hot throughout its entire area, or a badlands could have many chasms. In contrast, a heavy storm may stay only for a day.

A story needs beats to keep the viewer's hearts pumping, and as such travelling must do so as well. These can be combat / roleplay encounters, environments, tewists and setbacks, or simply setting the mood of the travel.

Wonder

Wonder is a large part of D&D, and when travel is commonly touted as being boring, then add some wonder back into it, Frieren style!

Mood

Set the mood of a certain part of the journet, one part that stands out, that gives a spark to the travel. For example, the creaking of splintery branches amidst the choking fog.

Small Jobs

An NPC needing a simple job can give your players a form of getting gold and make them feel like the good guys for helping out a stranger, or just give them someone to talk to.

Environments

Describing the environment immerses your players in what they're going through, it may prompt them to take up their shields when they feel threatened by the very trees, or to take in the wonder of the plantlife.

Random Encounters

Random encounters are easy ways to generate story beats for you, but there are times where you should slow down on them. I'd suggest 2 combat / environment encounters, with a roleplay encounter in the middle at the max, and if there's a large gap between where the players start and where they're going, give them towns in between for them to refill resources and do other things which have more tangibility within a defined location instead of a random shack on the road, perhaps getting other quests on their way.

When to Use / Importance

Although combat encounters can be fun, a random wolf encounter every few miles will drain just about any party; when the combat simply doesn't mean anything, why do it in the first place? The players have somewhere they need to go , where they want to be, so don't cram unnescesary encounters.

Combat Encounters

When things get heated, what are the enemies actually thinking? What are their goals? To what extent will they pursue these goals?

Roleplay Encounters

People like talking, so giving them an oppurtunity to do so is always pleasant to keep things light.

Environment Encounters

Creatures don't have to be the only things impeding the players, the world itself will gladly give them a reason to fear.
Supplies are often a very iffy part when it comes to almost every aspect of D&D due to their unnescesarry bloat, however, should your players (key point, your players!) want to handle supplies, there are some suggestions I can make, for instance, when on the road, instead of having the players go through all their things and individually work things out, you handle the supply drain yourself and report that back to the players, this way you may simply do everything in one quick motion; or, if they do not want to deal with that every time the have to travel, you may simply allow the players to describe some of these scenes, such as cooking food they gathered at the campfire.

Survival

Survivial doesn't need to mean adding a bunch of bloat mechanics, it can simply be the players playing their characters, what they do and add in between encounters.

Resources

Resources are more than rations, it can be a moment where your players can create and add to their character in ways not normally be allowed simply by levelling up. This can be crafting items, researching, hunting, getting a pet, or even improving their relations with NPCs.

Campfire Scenes

A marvellous question to ask your players is simply "what are your characters doing in this moment / as they are travelling?" and then go around the table, letting them decide what their characters would do (this also works many other times in the game!) This isn't necessarily mechanical, it can be purely decorative, but this allows them to play their character without going through the mechanics of base D&D.
